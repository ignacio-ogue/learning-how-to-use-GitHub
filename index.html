All Stakeholders

Every party involved in getting a prescription drug from approval to patient — and what role they play.

🧑‍⚕️
Patient
End Recipient
The person who receives the prescription and ultimately takes the medication. Navigates insurance requirements, copays, and pharmacy logistics.
May be covered by employer insurance, Medicare, Medicaid, or uninsured
👨‍⚕️
Prescriber
Initiates the Rx
Licensed healthcare provider who diagnoses the patient and writes the prescription. Can prescribe paper or electronically (e-Rx).
MD, DO, NP, PA, Dentist, Psychiatrist, Optometrist (varies by state)
💊
Pharmacy
Dispenses Drug
Receives the prescription, verifies it, checks for drug interactions, adjudicates with insurance, and dispenses the medication to the patient.
Retail (CVS, Walgreens), Mail-order, Specialty, Hospital, Compounding
🏢
PBM
Pharmacy Benefit Manager
Intermediary between insurers and pharmacies. Processes claims, negotiates drug prices, manages drug formularies, and operates mail-order pharmacies.
CVS Caremark, Express Scripts (Cigna), OptumRx (UnitedHealth)
🏦
Insurer / Payer
Covers Drug Cost
Pays the majority of covered drug costs. Sets formularies (covered drug lists), requires prior authorizations, and manages cost-sharing with patients.
Blue Cross, Aetna, United, Cigna, Medicare Part D, Medicaid
🚚
Wholesaler / Distributor
Middle of Supply Chain
Buys drugs from manufacturers in bulk and distributes to pharmacies and hospitals. The "invisible" logistics layer that keeps pharmacies stocked.
McKesson, AmerisourceBergen (Cencora), Cardinal Health (the "Big 3")
🏭
Drug Manufacturer
Produces the Drug
Researches, develops, and manufactures drugs. Sets the list (wholesale) price. May offer patient assistance programs or manufacturer coupons.
Pfizer, Eli Lilly, AbbVie (branded); Teva, Mylan (generic)
⚖️
FDA
Federal Regulator
Approves drugs for safety and efficacy before they reach the market. Regulates labeling, manufacturing standards, and post-market surveillance.
Reviews NDAs (new drugs), ANDAs (generics), and BLAs (biologics)
🏗️
GPO
Group Purchasing Org
Negotiates bulk drug prices on behalf of hospitals and health systems. Helps hospitals get better pricing than they could achieve individually.
Premier, Vizient, HealthTrust (HPG)
📋
Prior Authorization Team
Approval Gate
Clinical reviewers at insurance companies who evaluate whether a drug is medically necessary before approving coverage. Can approve, deny, or request more information.
Works within the insurer/PBM; may use third-party UM vendors

How Money Flows

The financial relationships in the prescription drug system are complex and often opaque. Here's who pays whom — and why.

Patient Cost-Sharing
Patient → Pharmacy
Patient pays a copay, coinsurance, or the full cash price at the counter. Copays range from $0 to hundreds of dollars depending on the drug tier.
Insurance Reimbursement
Insurer (via PBM) → Pharmacy
The insurer pays the pharmacy the contracted reimbursement rate, minus the patient's copay. This is adjudicated in real time at point of sale via the PBM.
Premiums & Funding
Employer / Government → Insurer
Employers pay premiums to fund employee health benefits. The government funds Medicare and Medicaid. This pool of money is what insurers use to pay drug claims.
Drug Purchase Cost
Pharmacy → Wholesaler
Pharmacies buy drugs from wholesalers at a negotiated price. Profit margins on drugs are often thin; pharmacies earn more on dispensing fees and front-of-store sales.
Wholesale Acquisition Cost (WAC)
Wholesaler → Manufacturer
Wholesalers pay manufacturers the WAC (list price minus any contract discounts). The Big 3 wholesalers earn thin margins (~1–2%) but on enormous volume.
Manufacturer Rebates
Manufacturer → PBM / Insurer
Manufacturers pay rebates to PBMs in exchange for favorable formulary placement. These rebates can be 20–50% of list price. A key driver of high list prices.
PBM Administrative Fees
Insurer → PBM
Insurers pay PBMs a per-member-per-month administrative fee to manage their pharmacy benefit. PBMs also profit by keeping a portion of manufacturer rebates.
340B Drug Pricing
Manufacturer → Safety-Net Hospitals
Qualifying hospitals (serving low-income patients) can buy drugs at heavily discounted 340B prices — sometimes 25–50% below WAC. A major source of hospital revenue.
⚠️ Why Drug Prices Are So High
The US drug pricing system creates a paradox: manufacturers set artificially high list prices to give room for rebates to PBMs. PBMs prefer high-rebate drugs, which incentivizes manufacturers to raise prices further. Patients who pay coinsurance (a % of list price) or are uninsured get hit hardest — they pay based on the inflated list price, not the net price after rebates. The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) began allowing Medicare to negotiate certain drug prices directly for the first time.
Glossary

Key terms you'll encounter when navigating the US prescription drug system.

Prior Authorization
PA
A requirement by the insurer that the prescriber obtain approval before a specific drug will be covered. Designed to ensure medical necessity and steer toward preferred drugs.
Pharmacy Benefit Manager
PBM
A third-party administrator that manages prescription drug benefits for insurers. Negotiates drug prices, processes claims, and manages formularies. Three PBMs control ~80% of the US market.
Formulary
Drug List
The list of prescription drugs covered by an insurance plan. Drugs are placed into "tiers" — lower tiers have lower copays. Non-formulary drugs may not be covered at all.
Step Therapy
Fail-First
A requirement that patients try cheaper or preferred drugs before the insurer will cover a more expensive drug. Patients must "fail" on the step-therapy drugs first.
Wholesale Acquisition Cost
WAC / List Price
The manufacturer's published list price for a drug — before any rebates or discounts. This is the baseline price from which all negotiations start. Usually much higher than what's actually paid.
Average Manufacturer Price
AMP
The average price manufacturers receive from wholesalers and direct purchasers. Used by Medicaid to calculate rebates. Lower than WAC due to volume discounts.
Electronic Prescription
e-Rx
A prescription sent digitally from the prescriber's EHR system directly to the pharmacy. Reduces errors, speeds processing, and is required for controlled substances in many states.
Specialty Pharmacy
A pharmacy that focuses on high-cost, complex drugs (biologics, infusibles). Provides additional patient support services, clinical monitoring, and handles special storage needs.
Patient Assistance Program
PAP
A manufacturer program that provides free or reduced-cost drugs to patients who cannot afford them. Often requires proof of income and lack of insurance coverage.
340B Drug Pricing Program
340B
A federal program requiring drug manufacturers to sell drugs at steeply discounted prices to qualifying "covered entities" such as safety-net hospitals and federally qualified health centers.
Diagnosis Related Group
DRG
A classification system used by Medicare to pay hospitals a fixed amount per inpatient stay based on diagnosis. Drug costs are bundled into the DRG payment — not billed separately.
Group Purchasing Organization
GPO
An entity that pools the purchasing power of hospitals and health systems to negotiate discounts from drug manufacturers and medical supply companies.
Biologic / Biosimilar
Biologics are large-molecule drugs derived from living cells (e.g., insulin, cancer immunotherapies). Biosimilars are near-identical copies of biologics, approved by FDA to be used interchangeably in many cases.
Medication Therapy Management
MTM
A service provided by pharmacists to optimize drug therapy for patients with complex conditions. Includes comprehensive medication reviews, action plans, and follow-up. Required under Medicare Part D.
